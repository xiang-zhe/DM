					<p>ISTANBUL, Turkey &#8212; A top conglomerate Thursday said it was in talks to sell Turkey&#8217;s largest media holding to a tycoon close to President Recep Tayyip Erdogan, raising fears of a new tightening of government control on the press.
<p>Dogan Holding said in a statement that talks had begun on the sale of Dogan Media Group to the Demiroren Group of magnate Erdogan Demiroren for around $1 billion (810 million euros).
							<p class="newsletter-promo">
								<a href="/signup/?utm_source=toi-articles&utm_medium=middle-link&utm_campaign=signup-buttons">
									Get The Times of Israel's Daily Edition by email and never miss our top stories
								</a>
								<a href="/signup/?utm_source=toi-articles&utm_medium=middle-link&utm_campaign=signup-buttons">
									Free Sign Up
								</a>
							</p>
<p>Dogan Media Group comprises some of Turkey&#8217;s most prestigious media names including the Hurriyet daily newspaper, the Fanatik sports daily, the CNN-Turk rolling news service and the Kanal-D channel.</p>
<p>The respected CNN-Turk and Hurriyet, while in no way anti-government, had been regarded as holding a relatively independent editorial line in recent years.</p>
<p>However, the Demiroren Holding is seen as more friendly to Erdogan.</p>
<p>The takeover plan, first reported by the T24 website on Wednesday, would see the government tightening its grip over the media at a hugely sensitive time ahead of presidential elections next year.</p>
<p>&#8220;By this huge takeover, the Turkish mass media industry comes under the direct political control of President Erdogan,&#8221; wrote prominent Turkish journalist Kadri Gursel, himself released from jail late last year, on Twitter.</p>

	<div id="attachment_1815408"  class="wp-caption  alignright"><img class="size-medium wp-image-1815408" src="https://static.timesofisrael.com/www/uploads/2018/03/AP_090220011531-400x250.jpg" alt="" width="400" height="250" />
		<div class="wp-caption-text">A TV transmission vehicle is parked outside of the Dogan group TV headquarters in Istanbul, Turkey, Friday, February 20, 2009. (AP Photo/Ibrahim Usta)</div>
	</div>
<p>Demiroren already bought the Milliyet and Vatan newspapers in 2011 from Dogan Holding, with the formerly mainstream outlets toeing the government line since then.</p>
<p>His son, Yildirim, also holds a powerful role as head of the Turkish Football Federation. He is a regular visitor to the presidential palace.</p>
<p>The Dogan Media Group is valued at $1.1 billion, although this is reduced to $890 million when debts are considered, according to the statement.</p>
<p>Company founder Aydin Dogan has had an uneasy relationship with Erdogan and his ruling Justice and Development Party (AKP) since the party came to power in 2002.</p>
<p>Dogan Holding, which also has interests in energy, trade and insurance, faced a record $2.5 billion in tax fines in 2009, sparking debate over government pressure on critical mainstream media.</p>
<p>Subsequently, the media group was forced to sell Milliyet and Vatan newspapers and the Star TV channel.</p>
<p>Observers have noted that in recent months Hurriyet has taken care not to irritate the government in its news stories, despite having some critical columnists.</p>
<p>Dozens of journalists have been jailed after the failed July 2016 coup aimed at unseating Erdogan and blamed by Ankara on US-based Muslim preacher Fethullah Gulen.</p>
