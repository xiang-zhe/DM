            Saudi crown prince says women need not wear head cover
        </h1>

            <p class="img-caption">Saudi crown prince Mohammad bin Salman said women need not wear the abaya robe or head cover, as long as their attire is &quot;decent and respectful&quot;</p>
        

        

        

        <!-- Main body content -->
        
        
            <p>Women in Saudi Arabia need not wear head cover or the black abaya - the full-length robes symbolic of Islamic piety - as long as their attire is &quot;decent and respectful&quot;, the kingdom&#39;s crown prince has said.</p>
<p>With the ascent to power of young Prince Mohammad bin Salman, the kingdom has seen an expansion in women&#39;s rights including a decision to allow women to attend mixed public sporting events and the right to drive cars from this summer.</p>
<p>The changes have been hailed as proof of a new progressive trend towards modernisation in the deeply conservative Muslim kingdom, although the gender-segregated nation continues to be criticised for its continued constraints on women.</p>
<p>&quot;The laws are very clear and stipulated in the laws of sharia: that women wear decent, respectful clothing, like men,&quot; Prince Mohammed said in an interview with CBS television aired late yesterday.</p>
<p>&quot;This, however, does not particularly specify a black abaya or a black head cover,&quot; he said.</p>
<p>&quot;The decision is entirely left for women to decide what type of decent and respectful attire she chooses to wear,&quot; he added.</p>
<p>A senior cleric said last month that women should dress modestly, but this did not necessitate wearing the abaya.</p>
<div class="ad-container"><amp-ad width="300" height="250" type="doubleclick" data-slot="/3014/RTE.ie/news/article"></amp-ad></div><p>It remains unclear if these statements signal a change in the enforcement of women&#39;s dress code in the kingdom.</p>
<p>Saudi Arabia has no written legal code to go with the texts making up sharia, and police and judiciary have long enforced a strict dress code requiring Saudi women to wear abayas, and in many cases to cover their hair and faces.</p>
<p>But the kingdom has witnessed a cautious new climate of social freedoms with the rise of the 32-year-old crown prince to power after decades of elderly rulers.</p>
<p>Saudi women have started wearing more colorful abayas in recent years, the light blues and pinks in stark contrast with the traditional black.</p>
<p>Open abayas over long skirts or jeans are also becoming more common in some parts of the country.</p>
<p>On 8 March, a group of women in the Saudi city of Jeddah marked International Women&#39;s Day by exercising one of their newly acquired freedoms: the right to go for a jog, paying no heed to bemused onlookers.</p>
<p>However, activists have blasted the country&rsquo;s continued guardianship system requiring a male family member to grant permission for a woman to study abroad, travel and other activities.</p>
<p>Last week, a UN rights watchdog called on Saudi Arabia to end discriminatory practices against women including male guardianship, and give them full access to justice.</p>
