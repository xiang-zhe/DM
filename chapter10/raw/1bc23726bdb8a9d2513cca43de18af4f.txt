		<h1 class="entry-title">China should prepare for military action over Taiwan: Chinese paper</h1>
	</header>
			<p class="entry-meta">
</p>
	<div class="entry-content clearfix">
    	<div style="float: left; width: 100%; font-size: small; padding: 0 0 20px 0; color: #999;"><img style="width:777px;height:auto;padding-bottom:0.5em;" src="http://d2pggiv3o55wnc.cloudfront.net/oann/wp-content/uploads/2018/03/2018-03-22T001031Z_1_LYNXMPEE2L00K_RTROPTP_0_TAIWAN-POLITICS_1.jpg" alt="FILE PHOTO: Members of the National Security Bureau take part in a drill at its headquarters in Taipei" /><br />FILE PHOTO: Members of the National Security Bureau take part in a drill next to a national flag at its headquarters in Taipei, Taiwan, November 13, 2015. REUTERS/Pichi Chuang</div>		<p>March 22, 2018</p>
<p>By Ben Blanchard</p>
<p>BEIJING (Reuters) &#8211; China should prepare for military action over self-ruled Taiwan and pressure Washington over cooperation on North Korea after the United States passed a law to boost ties with Taiwan, a widely read Chinese state-run newspaper said on Thursday.</p>
<p>Beijing was infuriated when U.S. President Donald Trump signed legislation last week that encourages the United States to send senior officials to Taiwan to meet Taiwanese counterparts and vice versa.</p>
<p>Beijing has also been upset by Trump&#8217;s threats to impose trade restrictions on China over its huge trade surplus with the United States.</p>
<p>U.S. Deputy Assistant Secretary of State Alex Wong said in Taipei on Wednesday the United States&#8217; commitment to Taiwan has never been stronger.</p>
<p>China claims Taiwan as its own and considers the self-ruled island a wayward province, which Chinese President Xi Jinping said on Tuesday would face the &#8220;punishment of history&#8221; for any attempt at separatism.</p>
<p>The Global Times said in an editorial China had to &#8220;strike back&#8221; against the U.S. law, for example by pressuring the United States in other areas of bilateral cooperation, like over North Korea and Iran.</p>
<p>&#8220;The mainland must also prepare itself for a direct military clash in the Taiwan Straits. It needs to make clear that escalation of U.S.-Taiwan official exchanges will bring serious consequences to Taiwan,&#8221; said the paper, which is published by the ruling Communist Party&#8217;s official People&#8217;s Daily.</p>
<p>&#8220;This newspaper has suggested that the mainland can send military planes and warships across the Taiwan Straits middle line. This can be implemented gradually depending on the cross-Straits situation,&#8221; it said.</p>
<p>Taiwan is one of China&#8217;s most sensitive issues and a potential military flashpoint. Underlining that threat, Taiwan sent ships and an aircraft on Wednesday to shadow a Chinese aircraft carrier group through the narrow Taiwan Strait, its defence ministry said.</p>
<p>Responding to requests for comment on the Global Times article and the carrier movement, a spokesman for the U.S. State Department said: &#8220;We oppose unilateral actions by either side to alter the status quo across the Strait.&#8221;</p>
<p>&#8220;The United States has a deep and abiding interest in cross-Strait peace and stability,&#8221; Justin Higgins added. &#8220;We welcome steps by both sides of the Taiwan Strait to reduce tensions and improve cross-Strait relations.&#8221;  </p>
<p>Underscoring China&#8217;s concerns, Taiwan&#8217;s government and the de facto U.S. embassy on the island said a second senior U.S. official would be visiting Taiwan this week, Deputy Assistant Secretary of Commerce for Manufacturing Ian Steff.</p>
<p>In Washington on Wednesday, the mayor of the Taiwanese city of Kaohsiung, Chen Chu, met with Susan Thornton, the U.S. State Department&#8217;s senior diplomat for East Asia, Taiwan&#8217;s representative office in Washington said.</p>
<p>Chen is a key ally of Taiwanese President Tsai Ing-wen, and a fellow member of the pro-independence Democratic Progressive Party.</p>
<p>In Beijing, foreign ministry spokeswoman Hua Chunying reiterated China&#8217;s opposition to official U.S.-Taiwan contacts, urging people to reread Xi&#8217;s comments from earlier in the week.</p>
<p>&#8220;The Chinese people share a common belief that it is never allowed, and it is absolutely impossible, to separate any inch of our great country&#8217;s territory from China,&#8221; Hua said, quoting Xi.</p>
<p>China&#8217;s hostility towards Taiwan has risen since Tsai&#8217;s 2016 election.</p>
<p>China suspects she wants to push for formal independence, which would cross a red line for Communist Party leaders in Beijing, though Tsai has said she wants to maintain the status quo and is committed to ensuring peace.</p>

<p> (Reporting by Ben Blanchard; Additional reporting by Fabian Hamacher and Twinnie Siu in TAIPEI and David Brunnstrom in WASHINGTON; Editing by Clarence Fernandez and James Dalgleish)</p>
