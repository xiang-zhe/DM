<div _ngcontent-c20="" innerhtml="<p>Poland has the <a href=&quot;http://databank.worldbank.org/data/download/GDP_PPP.pdf&quot; target=&quot;_blank&quot; data-ga-track=&quot;ExternalLink:http://databank.worldbank.org/data/download/GDP_PPP.pdf&quot; rel=&quot;nofollow&quot;>sixth-highest GDP (PPP)</a> in the European Union (EU) and is an increasingly important player in the world economy, with growing domestic and international reasons to tackle pollution. &amp;nbsp;Poland has the <a href=&quot;http://ec.europa.eu/eurostat/statistics-explained/index.php/Greenhouse_gas_emission_statistics_-_emission_inventories#Main_tables&quot; target=&quot;_blank&quot; data-ga-track=&quot;ExternalLink:http://ec.europa.eu/eurostat/statistics-explained/index.php/Greenhouse_gas_emission_statistics_-_emission_inventories#Main_tables&quot; rel=&quot;nofollow&quot;>fifth-highest greenhouse gas emissions</a> in the EU, behind only Germany, the UK, France, and Italy, and particulate pollution from fossil fuel combustion <a href=&quot;https://www.eea.europa.eu/publications/air-quality-in-europe-2017&quot; target=&quot;_blank&quot; data-ga-track=&quot;ExternalLink:https://www.eea.europa.eu/publications/air-quality-in-europe-2017&quot; rel=&quot;nofollow&quot;>causes </a><a href=&quot;https://www.eea.europa.eu/media/newsreleases/many-europeans-still-exposed-to-air-pollution-2015/premature-deaths-attributable-to-air-pollution&quot; target=&quot;_blank&quot; data-ga-track=&quot;ExternalLink:https://www.eea.europa.eu/media/newsreleases/many-europeans-still-exposed-to-air-pollution-2015/premature-deaths-attributable-to-air-pollution&quot; rel=&quot;nofollow&quot;>45,000 deaths each year</a><span> in Poland</span>.</p> 
<p>However, new analysis reveals a package of 10 well-designed policies can promote efficiency and renewable energy, helping to achieve Poland’s energy security goals, while lowering emissions and saving thousands of lives per year.</p> 
<p><strong>Poland Is A Lynchpin For European Climate Efforts</strong></p> 
<p>While most European countries have embraced strong emissions reductions targets, Poland has held back in recent years.&amp;nbsp; For example, in 2015, Poland <a href=&quot;https://www.reuters.com/article/us-poland-climatechange/polands-president-vetos-amendment-to-kyoto-protocol-on-co2-emissions-idUSKCN0SL1R020151027&quot; target=&quot;_blank&quot; data-ga-track=&quot;ExternalLink:https://www.reuters.com/article/us-poland-climatechange/polands-president-vetos-amendment-to-kyoto-protocol-on-co2-emissions-idUSKCN0SL1R020151027&quot; rel=&quot;nofollow&quot;>vetoed an amendment to the Kyoto Protocol</a> on carbon dioxide (CO<sub>2</sub>) emissions, thereby <a href=&quot;https://www.euractiv.com/section/climate-environment/news/eu-tempted-to-bypass-poland-to-meet-climate-commitments/&quot; target=&quot;_blank&quot; data-ga-track=&quot;ExternalLink:https://www.euractiv.com/section/climate-environment/news/eu-tempted-to-bypass-poland-to-meet-climate-commitments/&quot; rel=&quot;nofollow&quot;>preventing the 28-member EU from ratifying the treaty</a> as a bloc.&amp;nbsp; In 2016, Poland passed a <a href=&quot;http://midwestenergynews.com/2016/07/26/restrictive-new-law-will-harm-polands-wind-industry-advocates-say/&quot; target=&quot;_blank&quot; data-ga-track=&quot;ExternalLink:http://midwestenergynews.com/2016/07/26/restrictive-new-law-will-harm-polands-wind-industry-advocates-say/&quot; rel=&quot;nofollow&quot;>law that hinders wind energy development</a> by requiring setbacks of at least 10 times the turbine’s height from any buildings or forest, allowing for extended shutdowns for inspections, and authorizing a fourfold increase in taxes on wind farms.&amp;nbsp; In February 2018, Poland’s climate negotiator <a href=&quot;https://www.eenews.net/climatewire/2018/02/05/stories/1060072803&quot; target=&quot;_blank&quot; data-ga-track=&quot;ExternalLink:https://www.eenews.net/climatewire/2018/02/05/stories/1060072803&quot; rel=&quot;nofollow&quot;>called</a> for a focus on “poverty eradication, combating hunger, and security of energy policies” rather than a push for stronger national pledges to reduce pollution.</p> 
<p>Much of Poland’s lack of enthusiasm for climate policy can be explained by the political power and cultural salience of the national coal industry. Poland has a long history of mining and burning coal, a fuel that is seen as providing Poland with an <a href=&quot;http://midwestenergynews.com/2016/07/12/in-poland-efforts-to-rescue-coal-industry-will-likely-come-up-short/&quot; target=&quot;_blank&quot; data-ga-track=&quot;ExternalLink:http://midwestenergynews.com/2016/07/12/in-poland-efforts-to-rescue-coal-industry-will-likely-come-up-short/&quot; rel=&quot;nofollow&quot;>energy supply that is independent of Russia</a>, the source of most of Poland’s natural gas.</p> 
<p> 
 </p> 
<p>However, <a href=&quot;https://www.forbes.com/sites/energyinnovation/2017/11/20/coal-strikes-out-versus-cheap-renewables-but-winning-on-climate-may-require-a-policy-pinch-hitter/#7250ad378ac4&quot; target=&quot;_self&quot;>coal has long been on a downward trajectory</a> in Poland, as foreign countries with easily-accessed, higher-grade coal and more modern mining technology drove down global coal prices.&amp;nbsp; While the <a href=&quot;http://midwestenergynews.com/2016/07/12/in-poland-efforts-to-rescue-coal-industry-will-likely-come-up-short/&quot; target=&quot;_blank&quot; data-ga-track=&quot;ExternalLink:http://midwestenergynews.com/2016/07/12/in-poland-efforts-to-rescue-coal-industry-will-likely-come-up-short/&quot; rel=&quot;nofollow&quot;>Polish coal sector employed 400,000 people in 1989</a>, it employs only around 100,000 workers today, and that number is falling rapidly.</p> 
<p>Polish mining conglomerate PGG, the largest coal company in the EU, had to be <a href=&quot;http://www.dw.com/en/us-poland-energy-deal-bucks-anti-coal-trend/a-41419864&quot; target=&quot;_blank&quot; data-ga-track=&quot;ExternalLink:http://www.dw.com/en/us-poland-energy-deal-bucks-anti-coal-trend/a-41419864&quot; rel=&quot;nofollow&quot;>rescued from bankruptcy</a> in 2016. Poland now imports millions of tons of coal per year, and ironically, <a href=&quot;http://www.thenews.pl/1/12/Artykul/350038,Coal-imports-to-Poland-soar-Eurostat&quot; target=&quot;_blank&quot; data-ga-track=&quot;ExternalLink:http://www.thenews.pl/1/12/Artykul/350038,Coal-imports-to-Poland-soar-Eurostat&quot; rel=&quot;nofollow&quot;>most comes from Russia</a>. Facing the erosion of a domestic industry seen as insurance against dependence on Russia, it is not surprising that Poland has <a href=&quot;http://www.climatechangenews.com/2017/05/29/eu-climate-targets-undermined-polish-czech-revolt-documents-reveal/&quot; target=&quot;_blank&quot; data-ga-track=&quot;ExternalLink:http://www.climatechangenews.com/2017/05/29/eu-climate-targets-undermined-polish-czech-revolt-documents-reveal/&quot; rel=&quot;nofollow&quot;>pushed back against strong EU climate targets</a>, which will require a significant reduction in coal consumption.</p>

<p class=&quot;tweet_line&quot;><span class=&quot;tweet_quote&quot;> <a href=&quot;https://twitter.com/intent/tweet?url=http%3A%2F%2Fwww.forbes.com%2Fsites%2Fenergyinnovation%2F2018%2F03%2F20%2Fpolands-path-to-tackling-climate-change-40-fewer-emissions-26-billion-annual-savings-by-2050%2F&amp;amp;text=%23Poland%E2%80%99s%20significant%20coal%20use%2C%20the%20dominant%20CO2-emitting%20fuel%2C%20creates%20opportunities%20to%20cut%20emissions%20%40EnergyInnovLLC&quot; target=&quot;_blank&quot; data-ga-track=&quot;ExternalLink:https://twitter.com/intent/tweet?url=http%3A%2F%2Fwww.forbes.com%2Fsites%2Fenergyinnovation%2F2018%2F03%2F20%2Fpolands-path-to-tackling-climate-change-40-fewer-emissions-26-billion-annual-savings-by-2050%2F&amp;amp;text=%23Poland%E2%80%99s%20significant%20coal%20use%2C%20the%20dominant%20CO2-emitting%20fuel%2C%20creates%20opportunities%20to%20cut%20emissions%20%40EnergyInnovLLC&quot; rel=&quot;nofollow&quot;>Poland’s significant use of coal, the dominant CO2-emitting fuel, creates many opportunities to reduce emissions<span></span></a></span> . The <a href=&quot;https://www.forbes.com/sites/energyinnovation/2017/06/21/global-coal-use-power-sector-emissions-will-peak-within-a-decade-as-low-cost-renewable-energy-grows/#32739ef555a8&quot; target=&quot;_self&quot;>falling costs of solar panels and wind turbines</a> mean that Poland can make the transition to a low-carbon economy while saving money, as renewables have no fuel costs.&amp;nbsp; Renewables also improve energy security by obviating the need to import gas or coal from Russia.</p> 
<p>Recent news offers hope about Poland’s energy future. For example, PGE, the largest power company in Poland, announced plans to build <a href=&quot;https://www.statoil.com/en/news/05mar2018-offshore-wind-poland.html&quot; target=&quot;_blank&quot; data-ga-track=&quot;ExternalLink:https://www.statoil.com/en/news/05mar2018-offshore-wind-poland.html&quot; rel=&quot;nofollow&quot;>over 1,000 megawatts of offshore wind</a> capacity by 2030.</p> 
<p><strong>Poland's Greenhouse Gas Emissions</strong></p> 
<p>Electricity and industry are responsible for the lion’s share of emissions in Poland, making up 38 and 41% of the country’s emissions, respectively. Even without ambitious, new policy, the power sector is expected to decarbonize somewhat as old, dirty coal plants are replaced by newer coal and gas plants, and as solar gains a foothold.</p> 
<p>By 2050, about half of Poland’s total emissions are likely to come from the industrial sector, and the power sector will be responsible for about a quarter of the country’s total emissions. Buildings and transportation are about equal, and district heating emits about half as much as either one of these sectors, rounding out the rest of Poland’s emissions.</p>

<p>Industrial emissions are dominated by agriculture, natural gas, and petroleum systems, and chemicals – including the use of <a href=&quot;https://www.forbes.com/sites/energyinnovation/2018/02/27/global-cooling-is-on-the-brink-of-a-huge-economic-boom-which-countries-will-tap-the-opportunity/&quot; target=&quot;_self&quot;>refrigerants in air conditioners and refrigerators</a>. In the power sector, emissions are predominately from coal power plants.</p> 
<p><a href=&quot;https://www.forbes.com/sites/energyinnovation/2017/12/19/climate-change-and-global-poverty-can-only-be-solved-together/#41d4fd597cde&quot; target=&quot;_self&quot;>To decarbonize its economy</a>, Poland must focus on reducing emissions from these sources and others; but what policies can best achieve these reductions?</p>"><p class="speakable-paragraph">Poland has the <a href="http://databank.worldbank.org/data/download/GDP_PPP.pdf" target="_blank" data-ga-track="ExternalLink:http://databank.worldbank.org/data/download/GDP_PPP.pdf" rel="nofollow">sixth-highest GDP (PPP)</a> in the European Union (EU) and is an increasingly important player in the world economy, with growing domestic and international reasons to tackle pollution. &nbsp;Poland has the <a href="http://ec.europa.eu/eurostat/statistics-explained/index.php/Greenhouse_gas_emission_statistics_-_emission_inventories#Main_tables" target="_blank" data-ga-track="ExternalLink:http://ec.europa.eu/eurostat/statistics-explained/index.php/Greenhouse_gas_emission_statistics_-_emission_inventories#Main_tables" rel="nofollow">fifth-highest greenhouse gas emissions</a> in the EU, behind only Germany, the UK, France, and Italy, and particulate pollution from fossil fuel combustion <a href="https://www.eea.europa.eu/publications/air-quality-in-europe-2017" target="_blank" data-ga-track="ExternalLink:https://www.eea.europa.eu/publications/air-quality-in-europe-2017" rel="nofollow">causes </a><a href="https://www.eea.europa.eu/media/newsreleases/many-europeans-still-exposed-to-air-pollution-2015/premature-deaths-attributable-to-air-pollution" target="_blank" data-ga-track="ExternalLink:https://www.eea.europa.eu/media/newsreleases/many-europeans-still-exposed-to-air-pollution-2015/premature-deaths-attributable-to-air-pollution" rel="nofollow">45,000 deaths each year</a><span> in Poland</span>.</p> 
<p>However, new analysis reveals a package of 10 well-designed policies can promote efficiency and renewable energy, helping to achieve Poland’s energy security goals, while lowering emissions and saving thousands of lives per year.</p> 
<p><strong>Poland Is A Lynchpin For European Climate Efforts</strong></p> 
<p>While most European countries have embraced strong emissions reductions targets, Poland has held back in recent years.&nbsp; For example, in 2015, Poland <a href="https://www.reuters.com/article/us-poland-climatechange/polands-president-vetos-amendment-to-kyoto-protocol-on-co2-emissions-idUSKCN0SL1R020151027" target="_blank" data-ga-track="ExternalLink:https://www.reuters.com/article/us-poland-climatechange/polands-president-vetos-amendment-to-kyoto-protocol-on-co2-emissions-idUSKCN0SL1R020151027" rel="nofollow">vetoed an amendment to the Kyoto Protocol</a> on carbon dioxide (CO<sub>2</sub>) emissions, thereby <a href="https://www.euractiv.com/section/climate-environment/news/eu-tempted-to-bypass-poland-to-meet-climate-commitments/" target="_blank" data-ga-track="ExternalLink:https://www.euractiv.com/section/climate-environment/news/eu-tempted-to-bypass-poland-to-meet-climate-commitments/" rel="nofollow">preventing the 28-member EU from ratifying the treaty</a> as a bloc.&nbsp; In 2016, Poland passed a <a href="http://midwestenergynews.com/2016/07/26/restrictive-new-law-will-harm-polands-wind-industry-advocates-say/" target="_blank" data-ga-track="ExternalLink:http://midwestenergynews.com/2016/07/26/restrictive-new-law-will-harm-polands-wind-industry-advocates-say/" rel="nofollow">law that hinders wind energy development</a> by requiring setbacks of at least 10 times the turbine’s height from any buildings or forest, allowing for extended shutdowns for inspections, and authorizing a fourfold increase in taxes on wind farms.&nbsp; In February 2018, Poland’s climate negotiator <a href="https://www.eenews.net/climatewire/2018/02/05/stories/1060072803" target="_blank" data-ga-track="ExternalLink:https://www.eenews.net/climatewire/2018/02/05/stories/1060072803" rel="nofollow">called</a> for a focus on “poverty eradication, combating hunger, and security of energy policies” rather than a push for stronger national pledges to reduce pollution.</p> 
<p>Much of Poland’s lack of enthusiasm for climate policy can be explained by the political power and cultural salience of the national coal industry. Poland has a long history of mining and burning coal, a fuel that is seen as providing Poland with an <a href="http://midwestenergynews.com/2016/07/12/in-poland-efforts-to-rescue-coal-industry-will-likely-come-up-short/" target="_blank" data-ga-track="ExternalLink:http://midwestenergynews.com/2016/07/12/in-poland-efforts-to-rescue-coal-industry-will-likely-come-up-short/" rel="nofollow">energy supply that is independent of Russia</a>, the source of most of Poland’s natural gas.</p> 
<p> 
 </p> 
<p>However, <a href="https://www.forbes.com/sites/energyinnovation/2017/11/20/coal-strikes-out-versus-cheap-renewables-but-winning-on-climate-may-require-a-policy-pinch-hitter/#7250ad378ac4" target="_self">coal has long been on a downward trajectory</a> in Poland, as foreign countries with easily-accessed, higher-grade coal and more modern mining technology drove down global coal prices.&nbsp; While the <a href="http://midwestenergynews.com/2016/07/12/in-poland-efforts-to-rescue-coal-industry-will-likely-come-up-short/" target="_blank" data-ga-track="ExternalLink:http://midwestenergynews.com/2016/07/12/in-poland-efforts-to-rescue-coal-industry-will-likely-come-up-short/" rel="nofollow">Polish coal sector employed 400,000 people in 1989</a>, it employs only around 100,000 workers today, and that number is falling rapidly.</p> 
<p>Polish mining conglomerate PGG, the largest coal company in the EU, had to be <a href="http://www.dw.com/en/us-poland-energy-deal-bucks-anti-coal-trend/a-41419864" target="_blank" data-ga-track="ExternalLink:http://www.dw.com/en/us-poland-energy-deal-bucks-anti-coal-trend/a-41419864" rel="nofollow">rescued from bankruptcy</a> in 2016. Poland now imports millions of tons of coal per year, and ironically, <a href="http://www.thenews.pl/1/12/Artykul/350038,Coal-imports-to-Poland-soar-Eurostat" target="_blank" data-ga-track="ExternalLink:http://www.thenews.pl/1/12/Artykul/350038,Coal-imports-to-Poland-soar-Eurostat" rel="nofollow">most comes from Russia</a>. Facing the erosion of a domestic industry seen as insurance against dependence on Russia, it is not surprising that Poland has <a href="http://www.climatechangenews.com/2017/05/29/eu-climate-targets-undermined-polish-czech-revolt-documents-reveal/" target="_blank" data-ga-track="ExternalLink:http://www.climatechangenews.com/2017/05/29/eu-climate-targets-undermined-polish-czech-revolt-documents-reveal/" rel="nofollow">pushed back against strong EU climate targets</a>, which will require a significant reduction in coal consumption.</p>

The <a href="https://www.forbes.com/sites/energyinnovation/2017/06/21/global-coal-use-power-sector-emissions-will-peak-within-a-decade-as-low-cost-renewable-energy-grows/#32739ef555a8" target="_self">falling costs of solar panels and wind turbines</a> mean that Poland can make the transition to a low-carbon economy while saving money, as renewables have no fuel costs.&nbsp; Renewables also improve energy security by obviating the need to import gas or coal from Russia.</p> 
<p>Recent news offers hope about Poland’s energy future. For example, PGE, the largest power company in Poland, announced plans to build <a href="https://www.statoil.com/en/news/05mar2018-offshore-wind-poland.html" target="_blank" data-ga-track="ExternalLink:https://www.statoil.com/en/news/05mar2018-offshore-wind-poland.html" rel="nofollow">over 1,000 megawatts of offshore wind</a> capacity by 2030.</p> 
<p><strong>Poland's Greenhouse Gas Emissions</strong></p> 
<p>Electricity and industry are responsible for the lion’s share of emissions in Poland, making up 38 and 41% of the country’s emissions, respectively. Even without ambitious, new policy, the power sector is expected to decarbonize somewhat as old, dirty coal plants are replaced by newer coal and gas plants, and as solar gains a foothold.</p> 
<p>By 2050, about half of Poland’s total emissions are likely to come from the industrial sector, and the power sector will be responsible for about a quarter of the country’s total emissions. Buildings and transportation are about equal, and district heating emits about half as much as either one of these sectors, rounding out the rest of Poland’s emissions.</p>

Facing the erosion of a domestic industry seen as insurance against dependence on Russia, it is not surprising that Poland has &l;a href=\&q;http://www.climatechangenews.com/2017/05/29/eu-climate-targets-undermined-polish-czech-revolt-documents-reveal/\&q; target=\&q;_blank\&q;&g;pushed back against strong EU climate targets&l;/a&g;, which will require a significant reduction in coal consumption.\r\n\r\n[caption id=\&q;attachment_815\&q; photocredit=\&q;Wikimedia Commons, photo by Klaumich49, CC-BY-SA 4.0\&q;  align=\&q;\&q; width=\&q;1024\&q;]&l;img class=\&q;size-full wp-image-815\&q; src=\&q;http://blogs-images.forbes.com/energyinnovation/files/2018/03/So%C5%9Bnica-coal-mine-in-Gliwice.jpg?width=960\&q; alt=\&q;\&q; data-height=\&q;723\&q; data-width=\&q;1024\&q;&g; The So&a;#347;nica coal mine in Gliwice began operations in 1917. Polish mines suffer from years of under-investment and struggle to achieve profitability.[/caption]\r\n&l;p class=\&q;tweet_line\&q;&g;[tweet_quote display=\&q;#Poland&a;rsquo;s significant coal use, the dominant CO2-emitting fuel, creates opportunities to cut emissions @EnergyInnovLLC\&q;]Poland&a;rsquo;s significant use of coal, the dominant CO2-emitting fuel, creates many opportunities to reduce emissions[/tweet_quote] . The &l;a href=\&q;https://www.forbes.com/sites/energyinnovation/2017/06/21/global-coal-use-power-sector-emissions-will-peak-within-a-decade-as-low-cost-renewable-energy-grows/#32739ef555a8\&q;&g;falling costs of solar panels and wind turbines&l;/a&g; mean that Poland can make the transition to a low-carbon economy while saving money, as renewables have no fuel costs.&a;nbsp; Renewables also improve energy security by obviating the need to import gas or coal from Russia.\r\n\r\nRecent news offers hope about Poland&a;rsquo;s energy future. For example, PGE, the largest power company in Poland, announced plans to build &l;a href=\&q;https://www.statoil.com/en/news/05mar2018-offshore-wind-poland.html\&q; target=\&q;_blank\&q;&g;over 1,000 megawatts of offshore wind&l;/a&g; capacity by 2030.\r\n\r\n&l;strong&g;Poland&s;s Greenhouse Gas Emissions&l;/strong&g;\r\n\r\nElectricity and industry are responsible for the lion&a;rsquo;s share of emissions in Poland, making up 38 and 41% of the country&a;rsquo;s emissions, respectively. Even without ambitious, new policy, the power sector is expected to decarbonize somewhat as old, dirty coal plants are replaced by newer coal and gas plants, and as solar gains a foothold.\r\n\r\nBy 2050, about half of Poland&a;rsquo;s total emissions are likely to come from the industrial sector, and the power sector will be responsible for about a quarter of the country&a;rsquo;s total emissions. Buildings and transportation are about equal, and district heating emits about half as much as either one of these sectors, rounding out the rest of Poland&a;rsquo;s emissions.\r\n\r\n[caption id=\&q;attachment_816\&q; photocredit=\&q;Energy Innovation\&q;  align=\&q;\&q; width=\&q;762\&q;]&l;img class=\&q;size-full wp-image-816\&q; src=\&q;http://blogs-images.forbes.com/energyinnovation/files/2018/03/Poland-emissions-under-BAU.jpg?width=960\&q; alt=\&q;\&q; data-height=\&q;526\&q; data-width=\&q;762\&q;&g; Projection of Poland&s;s CO2e emissions by sector to 2050 under business-as-usual[/caption]\r\n\r\nIndustrial emissions are dominated by agriculture, natural gas, and petroleum systems, and chemicals &a;ndash; including the use of &l;a href=\&q;https://www.forbes.com/sites/energyinnovation/2018/02/27/global-cooling-is-on-the-brink-of-a-huge-economic-boom-which-countries-will-tap-the-opportunity/\&q;&g;refrigerants in air conditioners and refrigerators&l;/a&g;. In the power sector, emissions are predominately from coal power plants.\r\n\r\n&l;a href=\&q;https://www.forbes.com/sites/energyinnovation/2017/12/19/climate-change-and-global-poverty-can-only-be-solved-together/#41d4fd597cde\&q;&g;To decarbonize its economy&l;/a&g;, Poland must focus on reducing emissions from these sources and others; but what policies can best achieve these reductions?\r\n&l;!--nextpage--&g;\r\n&l;strong&g;Identifying The Best Climate Policies For Poland&l;/strong&g;\r\n\r\nTo help Poland achieve these aims, Energy Innovation partnered with the Polish National Energy Conservation Agency (KAPE) and the European Climate Foundation (ECF) to develop the &l;a href=\&q;https://poland.energypolicy.solutions/\&q; target=\&q;_blank\&q;&g;Poland Energy Policy Simulator&l;/a&g;&l;span&g; (EPS)&l;/span&g;.&a;nbsp; This peer-reviewed, free, and open-source tool, one of a &l;a href=\&q;https://www.energypolicy.solutions/\&q; target=\&q;_blank\&q;&g;series of simulators&l;/a&g; for countries around the world, will help Poland identify policies to accelerate a transition to a clean economy while maximizing financial benefits.\r\n\r\nThe Poland EPS allows anyone to construct packages of climate and energy policies to visualize their combined effects in Poland on pollutant emissions, financial costs and savings, human lives saved, the electricity system, and more.&a;nbsp; For example, a &l;a href=\&q;https://poland.energypolicy.solutions/scenarios/run?locale=en&a;amp;p1=0.7&a;amp;p2=0.2&a;amp;p3=0.4&a;amp;p13=0.4&a;amp;p15=0.4&a;amp;p34=0.5&a;amp;p36=0.21&a;amp;p46=0.08&a;amp;p47=0.08&a;amp;p48=0.08&a;amp;p49=0.08&a;amp;p50=0.08&a;amp;p51=0.08&a;amp;p52=0.08&a;amp;p53=0.08&a;amp;p56=0.7&a;amp;p58=0.7&a;amp;p68=0.75&a;amp;p150=0.4&a;amp;p152=0.4&a;amp;p156=0.4&a;amp;p158=0.4&a;amp;p186=200\&q; target=\&q;_blank\&q;&g;package of just 10 policies&l;/a&g; achieve an emissions reduction of more than 40% relative to 2005 levels by 2050, while reducing capital and operational costs throughout the economy by $26 billion in 2050.\r\n\r\n[caption id=\&q;attachment_817\&q; The bottom edge is Poland&a;rsquo;s trajectory under the set of 10 example policies.[/caption]\r\n\r\nThis modeling reveals several promising policies for decarbonizing Poland&a;rsquo;s economy:\r\n&l;/p&g;&l;ol&g;&l;li&g;&l;strong&g;Renewable portfolio standards &l;/strong&g;in the power sector can help drive more &l;a href=\&q;https://www.forbes.com/sites/energyinnovation/2017/06/08/the-iea-says-were-off-track-to-meet-climate-goals-lets-refocus-on-the-technologies-that-work/#51b8f09cccfb\&q;&g;zero-carbon electricity capacity&l;/a&g;, such as solar or wind, onto Poland&a;rsquo;s grid. These policies will be particularly important, as a significant share of Poland&a;rsquo;s coal capacity will retire in the near future and need to be replaced. Poland&a;rsquo;s grid is somewhat small and &l;a href=\&q;http://forum-energii.eu/en/news/res_integration.html\&q; target=\&q;_blank\&q;&g;inflexible&l;/a&g;, and additional measures that add to &l;a href=\&q;http://energyinnovation.org/wp-content/uploads/2017/10/A-Roadmap-For-Finding-Flexibility-In-Wholesale-Power-Markets_FINAL.pdf\&q; target=\&q;_blank\&q;&g;grid flexibility&l;/a&g; &a;ndash; such as expanded transmission, demand response, energy storage, and fast ramping supply &a;ndash; can help integrate a growing share of renewables.&l;/li&g;\r\n \t&l;li&g;&l;strong&g;Industry energy efficiency standards,&l;/strong&g; especially for the chemicals and natural gas and petroleum industries, can help reduce the energy demanded by Poland&a;rsquo;s industry sector. Complementary policies supporting combined heat and power, waste heat recovery, and improved design of industrial facilities can further lower energy consumption.&l;/li&g;\r\n \t&l;li&g;&l;strong&g;Building energy efficiency standards (efficient building codes)&l;/strong&g;, like standards in industry, play a large role in cost-effectively reducing emissions, primarily by preventing thermal losses through the building envelope and improving the efficiency of furnaces and water heaters.&l;/li&g;\r\n \t&l;li&g;&l;strong&g;Vehicle fuel economy standards&l;/strong&g; are particularly important in reducing oil consumption, saving money, and reducing emissions. In the long term, vehicle electrification will likely dominate on-road transportation, so fuel economy standards are most crucial over the next few decades.&l;/li&g;\r\n \t&l;li&g;&l;strong&g;Capture and destruction of methane&l;/strong&g; can address fugitive emissions from the natural gas industry, which is one of the largest sources of non-CO&l;sub&g;2&l;/sub&g; greenhouse gases in Poland.&l;/li&g;\r\n \t&l;li&g;&l;strong&g;Elimination of high global warming potential gases used as refrigerants and coolants &l;/strong&g;offer a cost-effective opportunity to reduce emissions.
