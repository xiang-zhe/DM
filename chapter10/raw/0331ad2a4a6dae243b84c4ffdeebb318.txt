      <img src="http://static1.businessinsider.com/image/5a661c1700d0efef018b470c-1743/rts12tf7%201.jpg" alt="mark zuckerberg" data-mce-source="Stephen Lam/Reuters" data-mce-caption="Facebook Founder and CEO Mark Zuckerberg speaks on stage during the annual Facebook F8 developers conference in San Jose, California, U.S., April 18, 2017."><span class="caption-source">

  </p>
  <ul>
<li>
      <strong>Facebook executives waded into a firestorm of criticism
    on Saturday, after news reports revealed that a data firm with
    ties to the Trump campaign harvested private information from
    millions of Facebook users.</strong>
    </li>
    <li>
      <strong>Several executives took to Twitter to insist that the
    data leak was not technically a "breach."</strong>
    </li>
    <li>
      <strong>But critics were outraged by the response and accused
    the company of playing semantics and missing the
    point.</strong>
    </li>
  </ul>
<hr>
<p>
  Facebook and its executives faced a torrent of backlash on
  Saturday following news reports that the data firm Cambridge
  Analytica, which worked on the Trump campaign in 2016, <a href="http://www.businessinsider.com/cambridge-analytica-trump-firm-facebook-data-50-million-users-2018-3" target="_blank" rel="noopener">improperly harvested private
  information</a> from 50 million Facebook users.
</p>
  <p>
  The company quickly faced calls for increased regulation and
  oversight, and Massachusetts' Attorney General, Maura Healey,
  even announced an investigation.
</p>
  <p>
  "Massachusetts residents deserve answers immediately from
  Facebook and Cambridge Analytica," <a href="https://twitter.com/MassAGO/status/975052674818347013" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Healey said on Twitter</a>.
</p>
  <p>
  Sen. Amy Klobuchar of Minnesota also excoriated the company,
  demanding that Facebook CEO Mark Zuckerberg face the Senate
  Judiciary Committee for questioning.
</p>
  <p>
  "This is a major breach that must be investigated. It's clear
  these platforms can't police themselves," <a href="https://twitter.com/amyklobuchar/status/975158333446541312" target="_blank" rel="noopener">she said on Twitter</a>. "I've
  called for more transparency &amp; accountability for online
  political ads. They say 'trust us.' Mark Zuckerberg needs to
  testify before Senate Judiciary."
</p>
  <p>
  But much of the online outrage came after multiple Facebook
  executives took to Twitter to respond to the news reports,
  insisting the incident was not a "data breach."
</p>
  <p>
  "This was unequivocally not a data breach," longtime Facebook
  executive Andrew Bosworth <a href="https://twitter.com/boztank/status/975018461997887494" target="_blank" rel="noopener">tweeted</a>. "People chose to share their
  data with third party apps and if those third party apps did not
  follow the data agreements with us/users it is a violation. no
  systems were infiltrated, no passwords or information were stolen
  and hacked."
</p>
  <p>
  In a <a href="https://twitter.com/aprilaser/status/975078309930311680" target="_blank" rel="noopener">series of tweets</a> that have since been
  deleted, Facebook's chief security officer, Alex Stamos, insisted
  that although user's personal information may have been misused,
  it wasn't retroactively a "breach."
</p>
  <p>
  "The recent Cambridge Analytica stories by the NY Times and The
  Guardian are important and powerful, but it is incorrect to call
  this a "breach" under any reasonable definition of the term,"
  Stamos tweeted. "We can condemn this behavior while being
  accurate in our description of it."
</p>
  <p>
  Twitter users were not satisfied with that response  many
  accused the executives of deliberately missing the point by
  focusing on semantics.
</p>
