  <p>Space junk is a real problem that could trap humans forever on Earth — so now Australian scientists are working on powerful ground-based lasers to shoot it all away.</p><div class="inline-content wysiwyg right">
<div class="inner">
  <h2>Key points:</h2><ul><li>Laser will be able track debris and stop them from colliding by moving them</li><li>Dr Greene says multiple satellites are lost each each to space debris</li><li>In Europe space harpoons and space nets are being developed for catching larger pieces</li></ul> </div>
</div><p> </p><p>EOS Space Systems has been building and developing tracking systems for satellites and space debris at Mount Stromlo in Canberra.</p><p>And the company's CEO, Professor Craig Smith, says researchers have developed a photon pressure laser that "is able to nudge space debris objects around, change their orbits."</p><p>The first step for scientists would be to use low-powered lasers to detect and follow identified objects in space.</p><p>"With the laser tracking systems we track objects and predict collisions to high accuracy," Professor Smith said.</p><p>If a piece of space debris then looked like it could have a collision with another piece, then a laser would be used to change its orbit.</p><p>The next step would be to keep "ramping up the power to bigger and bigger lasers."</p><div class="inline-content photo full">
    <a href="/news/2018-03-21/space-junk-map/9573564">











  
  
  
    
    
    
    
    
    
    
    
    
  <img src="http://www.abc.net.au/news/image/8973098-3x2-700x467.jpg" alt="Computer generated image of the amount of space junk near earth." title="Space junk map" width="700" height="467"/>
</a><a href="/news/2018-03-21/space-junk-map/9573564" class="inline-caption"><strong>
        Photo:</strong>
       Computer generated image of objects in Earth orbit that are currently being tracked. <span class="source">(NASA)</span>
      </a></div><p> </p><p>"Then we can start moving it enough to what we call 'de-orbit' the satellite by reducing its velocity enough that it starts to change orbit height.</p><blockquote class="quote--pullquote"><p>"It eventually hits the atmosphere and the atmosphere takes over and drags it and burns it up." </p></blockquote><p>However, Professor Smith said scientists would have to ensure they did not break debris into two, which would then only add to the amount of pieces in orbit.</p><p>"The real trouble is you make it harder. If they get smaller they get harder and harder to track, and so they start to become invisible — but also still lethal to satellites."</p><h2>Australia 'is best place for laser technology'</h2><p>Space junk might sound innocuous enough, but as the Hollywood film Gravity reminded cinema-goers it can be a killer — especially if you are an astronaut hanging about in space and getting in its way.</p><p>Equally it can also destroy satellites, of which there are many circling the Earth.</p><p>Dr Ben Greene, the chairman of the Space Environment Research Centre, said there were "hundreds and thousands of space debris that are a size where they would threaten satellites."</p><blockquote class="quote--pullquote"><p>"We're losing multiple satellites a year now to space debris," he said.</p></blockquote><div class="inline-content photo full">
    <a href="/news/2018-03-21/sky-muster-satellite/9573578">











  
  
  
    
    
    
    
    
    
    
    
    
  <img src="http://www.abc.net.au/news/image/7795480-3x2-700x467.jpg" alt="Artist&#039;s impression of the Sky Muster satellite" title="Sky Muster satellite" width="700" height="467"/>
</a><a href="/news/2018-03-21/sky-muster-satellite/9573578" class="inline-caption"><strong>
        Photo:</strong>
       Satellites often fall victim to space debris. <span class="source">(Supplied: NBN Co)</span>
      </a></div><p> </p><p>Under the worst-case scenario for space debris, a cascading series of collisions could render the near-Earth orbit a no-go zone for satellites and space craft.</p><p>Hence the urgency in finding ways to get rid of space junk. </p><p>Efforts are underway to make space flight less messy, such as eliminating spent rocket boosters which are currently dumped in space.</p><p>In Europe, <a href="/news/2018-03-16/space-junk-the-target-of-new-airbus-project/9556210" target="_self" title="">space harpoons</a> and space nets are being developed for catching larger pieces of debris. </p><p>But right here in Australia the focus has been on using concentrated light to nudge the space junk out of harm's way.</p><p>And Dr Greene said Australia was the obvious place to station the laser technology.</p><p>"It's most likely going to be from Australia, because you need a decent-seized platform to work from and you need good weather. And Australia has both," he said.</p>
