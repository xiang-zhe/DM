style="font-size:1.2em"><font><font>Twenty aircraft were built including six prototypes and development aircraft.</font></font></font></p><p class="mol-para-with-font"><font style="font-size:1.2em">Air France (AF) and British Airways (BA) each received seven aircraft. </font></p><p class="mol-para-with-font"><font style="font-size:1.2em">The research and development failed to make a profit and the two airlines bought the aircraft at a huge discount.</font></p><p class="mol-para-with-font"><font style="font-size:1.2em">Among other destinations, Concorde flew regular transatlantic flights from London Heathrow and Paris Charles de Gaulle Airport to New York-JFK, Washington Dulles and Barbados.</font></p><p class="mol-para-with-font"><font style="font-size:1.2em">It flew these routes in less than half the time of other airliners. </font></p><p class="mol-para-with-font"><font style="font-size:1.2em">Over time, the aircraft became profitable when it found a customer base willing to pay for flights on what was for most of its career the fastest commercial airliner in the world.</font></p><p class="mol-para-with-font"><font style="font-size:1.2em"><font><font>The aircraft is regarded by many as an aviation icon and an engineering marvel, but it was also criticized for being uneconomical, lacking a credible market, and consuming more fuel to carry fewer passengers than a Boeing 747.</font></font></font></p><p class="mol-para-with-font"><font style="font-size:1.2em">Concorde was retired in 2003 due to a general downturn in the commercial aviation industry after the type's only crash in 2000, the September 11 attacks in 2001, and a decision by Airbus, the successor to Aérospatiale and BAC, to discontinue maintenance support.</font></p><div class="moduleFull mol-video"><div class="item"> <div id="p-54" class="sciencetech x-wide video_carousel_container" style="width:964px;"
