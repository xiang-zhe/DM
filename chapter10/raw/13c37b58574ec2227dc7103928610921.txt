He faced a major setback Thursday when a court in the northeastern city of Vaslui refused to overturn his death certificate because his request was filed "too late."</p><figure class="_3jvnrUdBzzciqbdDNc72hX bhq_VSwN8mlY40hZK4tid"><picture class="_1sY8sVdb57K64rzlUznfKp"><img src="https://media4.s-nbcnews.com/j/newscms/2018_11/2365906/180316-constantin-reliu-ac-530p_beb5e6f30adf7497afb04d9d3532f37c.fit-360w.jpg" alt=""/></picture><figcaption class="_1mNthW7mqFJbi97JOX5IHq"> <!-- -->Constantin Reliu poses for a portrait at his home on March 16, 2018 in the eastern town of Barlad.<!-- --> <span class="_1_nNY9NTCFD3zU0tnwCGFD">Adrian Arnautu / AFP - Getty Images</span></figcaption></figure><p>The decision, the court said, is final.</p><p>"I am a living ghost," Reliu told The Associated Press in a phone interview Friday from his home in Barlad, northeastern Romania.</p><div class="Hu6r6bzzUDkhbCzEiWRZD ad-container"><div></div></div><p>"I am officially dead, although I'm alive," he said. "I have no income and because I am listed as dead, I can't do anything."</p><p>During the interview, Reliu was deeply emotional, starting off by saying "I think I am going to cry" and going on to voice rage and a desire for revenge against his wife, who now lives in Italy.</p><p>"I am not sure whether I am divorced or not," he said. "I am not sure whether she is married to someone else or not. Nobody will tell me."</p><p>Reliu explained that he first went to work in Turkey in 1992 and returned in 1995 to the first big shock of his marriage — his wife's infidelity. In 1999, he decided to return to Turkey for good.</p><div class="Hu6r6bzzUDkhbCzEiWRZD ad-container"><div></div></div><p>The AP was not able to locate his wife to hear her side of the story.</p><p>Last December Turkish authorities detained him over expired papers and in January deported him to Romania.</p><p>Upon landing at Bucharest airport, he was informed by border officials that he had been officially declared dead and underwent six hours of questioning and tests.</p><p>They measured the distance between his eyes to see if it corresponded to an old passport photograph; they asked him questions about his home town, such as where the town hall was; they checked his fingerprints.</p><div class="Hu6r6bzzUDkhbCzEiWRZD ad-container"><div></div></div><p>"They decided that it was me!" he said.</p><p>But authorities in Barlad were less convinced.
