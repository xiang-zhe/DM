  <p>Only half of all Australians brush their teeth twice a day and it's contributing to high levels of decay across the country, a report card on the nation's dental health has found.</p><div class="inline-content wysiwyg right">
<div class="inner">
  <h2>Key points:</h2><ul><li>90 per cent of Australian adults have some form of tooth decay, Oral Health Tracker data finds</li><li>Report highlights how much smoking, drinking contribute to poor oral health</li><li>Data also found almost three-quarters of children eat too much sugar</li></ul></div>
</div><p> </p><p>A leading think-tank has published the country's first comprehensive Oral Health Tracker and found a series of damning statistics about the state of the population's teeth.</p><p>It's found 90 per cent of adults have some form of tooth decay, and risky alcohol consumption and smoking habits are contributing to poor oral health.</p><p>The Australian Health Policy Collaboration data also found almost three-quarters of children eat too much sugar and <a href="/news/2018-02-14/dentists-are-fed-up-with-doing-tooth-extractions-on-toddlers/9425958" target="_self" title="">more than one-third of five-year-olds had decay in baby teeth</a>.</p><p>Too many youngsters are also ending up in hospital with preventable dental problems, with children aged five to nine having the highest rates of admissions.</p><div class="inline-content photo right">
    <a href="/news/2018-03-20/person-with-gum-disease-and-tooth-decay/9562744">











  
  
  
    
    
    
    
    
    
    
    
    
  <img src="http://www.abc.net.au/news/image/9562692-3x2-340x227.jpg" alt="Person with gum disease and tooth decay" title="Person with gum disease and tooth decay" width="340" height="227"/>
</a><a href="/news/2018-03-20/person-with-gum-disease-and-tooth-decay/9562744" class="inline-caption"><strong>
        Photo:</strong>
       Experts say dental care needs to be considered as a part of overall health care. <span class="source">(Supplied)</span>
      </a></div><p> </p><p>The Oral Health Tracker also found about one-quarter of teens had not had a dental check-up in 12 months.</p><p>Adults aged 18 to 35 and women were more likely to feature in the 51 per cent of adults who brush their teeth twice a day, with brushing habits dropping off as people aged.</p><p>Among adults, 15.5 per cent had severe tooth loss — meaning they had fewer than 21 teeth, rather than a full set of 32.</p><p>Professor Rosemary Calder is the director of the Australian Health Policy Collaboration and said the figures showed, "We're not doing terribly well as a nation".</p><blockquote class="quote--pullquote"><p>"We've got a lot of preventable chronic disease, which is making us a very unwell nation and it's only going to get worse," she said.</p></blockquote><p>"Very few people really understand that oral health links directly to physical and mental health."</p><div class="inline-content photo right">
    <a href="/news/2018-03-20/kids-teeth-decay/9562736">











  
  
  
    
    
    
    
    
    
    
    
    
  <img src="http://www.abc.net.au/news/image/8720922-3x2-340x227.png" alt="Sugary foods were a major cause of decay, Dr Penrose said." title="Childhood tooth decay" width="340" height="227"/>
</a><a href="/news/2018-03-20/kids-teeth-decay/9562736" class="inline-caption"><strong>
        Photo:</strong>
       More than one-third of five-year-olds have decay in baby teeth. <span class="source">(Supplied: SA Health)</span>
      </a></div><p> </p><p>She said the statistics around child dental health were most troubling.</p><p>"Clearly we need to see how we can do better for Australia's children," Professor Calder said.</p><p>The president of the Australian Dental Association, Dr Hugo Sachs, said rates of dental decay were rising and a community-wide response was needed.</p><p>"We spend millions if not billions of dollars on unnecessary dentistry in this country," he said. "There are vulnerable Australians that can't gain access and something has to be done."</p><div class="inline-content wysiwyg full">
<div class="inner">
  <strong>Do you know more about this story? Email <a href="mailto:Specialist.Team@abc.net.au" target="_blank" title="">Specialist.Team@abc.net.au</a></strong></div>
</div><p> </p><h2>Smoking, drinking, sugar are all bad for teeth</h2><p>The report also highlights how much smoking and drinking are contributing to poor levels of oral health.</p><div class="inline-content wysiwyg right">
<div class="inner">
  <h2><a href="/news/2016-03-17/quiz-what-does-six-teaspoons-of-sugar-look-like/7086790" target="_self" title="">Can you beat our sugar quiz?</a></h2><a href="/news/2016-03-17/quiz-what-does-six-teaspoons-of-sugar-look-like/7086790" target="_self" title=""><img alt="" class="float--none" src="/cm/lb/7255896/data/sugar-promo-not-for-reuse-340-x-180-data.jpg"/> </a> <br/><a href="/news/2016-03-17/quiz-what-does-six-teaspoons-of-sugar-look-like/7086790" target="_self" title="">We're supposed to limit our sugar to six teaspoons a day, but do you know how much of it is hiding in some of our most popular groceries?</a></div>
</div><p> </p><p>It showed 17.1 per cent of Australians engaged in "risky" drinking, which contributes to decay and is linked to some forms of cancer.</p><p>While smoking rates are dropping, there's still 12.2 per cent of the population who are identified as smokers.</p><p>"It can get as bad as oral cancers and that leads to premature death," Professor Calder said.</p><p>Dr Sachs said many people still did not realise the link between sugar consumption and tooth decay.</p><p>"A lot of people don't go to the dentist until they have a significant problem," he said.</p><blockquote class="quote--pullquote"><p>"The reality is that if they attended regularly and undertook good oral hygiene, reduced their sugar intake and visited their dentist at least twice a year, a lot of these problems would disappear."</p></blockquote><h2>Better food labelling and more research needed: experts</h2><p>The experts said state and federal governments needed to fund more community education programs, particularly in disadvantaged areas.</p><p>They also said a program similar to the Child Dental Benefits Schedule was needed for the elderly.</p><p>Under this scheme, children whose parents qualified for Family Tax Benefit A or a government payment could get up to $1,000 free dental treatment.</p><div class="inline-content photo right">
    <a href="/news/2018-03-20/dr-hugo-sachs,-president-of-the-australian-dental-association/9562810">











  
  
  
    
    
    
    
    
    
    
    
    
  <img src="http://www.abc.net.au/news/image/9562804-3x2-340x227.jpg" alt="Dr Hugo Sachs, president of the Australian Dental Association" title="Dr Hugo Sachs, president of the Australian Dental Association" width="340" height="227"/>
</a><a href="/news/2018-03-20/dr-hugo-sachs,-president-of-the-australian-dental-association/9562810" class="inline-caption"><strong>
        Photo:</strong>
       Dr Hugo Sachs said rates of dental decay were rising. <span class="source">(ABC News: Alison Branley)</span>
      </a></div><p> </p><p>They also wanted better labelling on foods so consumers could understand just how much sugar was in particular products.</p><p>"Packaging of goods doesn't really declare the added sugars that occur within products and some of those are considered to be healthy," Dr Sachs said.</p><p>Professor Calder said dental care needed to be considered as a part of overall health care.</p><p>"If we don't improve this then we're going to pay for it through hospital care," she said.</p><p>Dr Sachs added that dental research was also grossly underfunded.</p><blockquote class="quote--pullquote"><p>"Less than 0.5 per cent of research dollars goes into dentistry yet it is the most common preventable disease in Australia," he said.</p></blockquote><p>The Oral Health Tracker has been developed by dental academics, researchers, clinicians, policy and public health experts.</p><p>They've set 15 targets for adults and young people's dental health, and plan to regularly report how Australia's dental health is tracking.</p>
