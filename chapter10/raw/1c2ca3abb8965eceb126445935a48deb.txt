			<p>Israel&#8217;s Justice Ministry has informed Facebook that it is opening an &#8220;administrative investigation&#8221; into the company following reports of the transfer of personal information from Facebook to data-mining firm Cambridge Analytics &#8220;and the possibility of additional violations of Israelis&#8217; personal information,&#8221; the ministry says.</p>

			<h4>Poland puts Berlin&#8217;s WWII bill at 440 billion euros</h4> 
			<p>WARSAW, Poland &#8212; Polish victims of World War II should be able to claim 440 billion euros ($543 billion) from Germany in World War II damages, the head of a parliamentary commission says Thursday.</p>
<p>Arkadiusz Mularczyk of the nationalist ruling Law and Justice Party (PiS) bases the figure, which the German government rejects, on a 1990 study which found that &#8220;there were 13.32 million direct victims of Nazi Germany in Poland.</p>
<p>That figure takes into account deportees, labor and concentration camp prisoners, civilians left maimed during that period, widows, orphans, and those who suffered from illnesses which broke out under Nazi occupation and during the conflict.</p>
<p>Mularczyk says the 1990 study found that reparations of $284 billion should be paid.</p>
<p>&#8220;That was the sum in 1990. If we revalue taking into account dollar purchasing power in 2017 we get 1.984 trillion zloty, or $543 billion,&#8221; the PAP news agency quotes Mularczyk as saying.</p>
<p>The issue of German reparations had long been regarded as closed &#8212; but PiS leader Jaroslaw Kaczynski revived the topic last year.</p>
<p>Last year, then German government spokesman Steffen Seibert said the issue had already been settled in August 1953 when Poland&#8217;s then communist administration said it renounced any right to war reparations.</p>
<p><em>&#8212; AFP</em></p>

			<a href="https://www.timesofisrael.com/liveblog_entry/israel-opens-investigation-into-facebook-for-cambridge-analytica-data-breach/"><span data-timestamp="1521728845" title="Thu, 22 Mar 2018 16:27:25 +0200">4:27 pm</span></a>

		<div class="liveblog-paragraph">
			<h4>Israel opens investigation into Facebook for Cambridge Analytica data breach</h4> 
			<p>Israel&#8217;s Justice Ministry has informed Facebook that it is opening an &#8220;administrative investigation&#8221; into the company following reports of the transfer of personal information from Facebook to data-mining firm Cambridge Analytics &#8220;and the possibility of additional violations of Israelis&#8217; personal information,&#8221; the ministry says.</p>
<p>Cambridge Analytica been under fire since The New York Times and The Guardian newspaper reported that it used data inappropriately obtained from roughly 50 million Facebook users to try to influence elections, including the 2016 US presidential election. Among that information were users&#8217; likes.</p>
<p>Additionally, a report by UK&#8217;s Channel 4 revealed hidden camera footage in which Cambridge Analytica&#8217;s chief executive Alexander Nix describes using Israeli &#8220;intelligence gathering&#8221; to get information about voters.</p>
<p>&#8220;According to privacy laws, personal information may only be used for the purpose it was given over for and may be transferred to another party only with consent,&#8221; a statement from the Justice Ministry&#8217;s Privacy Protection Authority statement says.</p>
<p>&#8220;The Authority will therefore investigate whether personal information of Israeli users has been illegally used in a manner that violates their right to privacy and violates the laws of privacy protection in Israel,&#8221; the statement adds.</p>
<p>Yesterday, breaking five days of silence, Facebook CEO Mark Zuckerberg apologized for the &#8220;major breach of trust,&#8221; admitted mistakes and outlined steps to protect user data.</p>
<p>A spokesperson for the Justice Ministry confirms to the Times of Israel that the probe is not currently being treated as a criminal investigation, but says it may become one &#8220;at a later date.&#8221;</p>
<p><em>&#8212; Raoul Wootliff</em></p>
