react-text: 177 --><!-- /react-text --><div class="PostContent" data-reactid="178"><div data-reactid="179"><p><u>An Israeli military court</u> sentenced Ahed Tamimi, a teenage Palestinian activist, to <a href="https://www.haaretz.com/israel-news/palestinian-teen-ahed-tamimi-reaches-plea-bargain-to-serve-8-months-i-1.5933423">eight months in jail</a> on Wednesday for slapping an Israeli soldier during a confrontation outside her family home in the occupied West Bank that was <a href="https://www.facebook.com/nariman.tamimi.1/videos/1941041279245238/">streamed live on Facebook</a>. The girl&#8217;s mother, Nariman, was convicted of incitement for sharing the recording online, and also sentenced to eight months in prison.</p>
<p>The video, viewed millions of times on social networks, prompted <a href="https://theintercept.com/2017/12/20/israel-tackles-existential-threat-posed-16-year-old-palestinian-girl/">an outcry from the Israeli public</a> at the supposed humiliation of a heavily armed soldier, tasked with enforcing his nation&#8217;s military rule over Palestinian civilians in the occupied West Bank, backing away from a physical confrontation with a 16-year-old girl.</p>

<iframe src="https://www.facebook.com/plugins/video.php?href=https%3A%2F%2Fwww.facebook.com%2Fnariman.tamimi.1%2Fvideos%2F1941041279245238%2F&amp;show_text=0&amp;width=560" width="560" height="315" style="border:none;overflow:hidden" scrolling="no" frameborder="0" allowtransparency="true" allowfullscreen="true" class="iframe-class"></iframe>

<p>The mother and daughter have both been held in pretrial detention since December.</p>
<p>Gaby Lasky, the Tamimis&#8217;s lawyer, <a href="https://972mag.com/ahed-tamimis-lawyer-her-case-is-making-people-see-the-occupation-again/132189/">told the Israeli magazine +972</a> in January that even charging Ahed&#8217;s mother with incitement for her live video broadcast was &#8220;very dangerous for freedom of the press.&#8221; Nariman had begun filming the soldier and a colleague standing in her family&#8217;s backyard several minutes before her daughter and a cousin approached them and tried to shove the Israelis off the property.</p>
<p>The ruling could have a chilling effect not just on journalists, but also on Palestinian activists who routinely <a href="https://thelede.blogs.nytimes.com/2011/06/17/palestinians-film-west-bank-protests-with-israeli-supplied-cameras/">record their encounters with Israeli troops</a>, both to document abuses and to provide a measure of accountability. Several years ago, the Israeli rights group B&#8217;Tselem, which works to monitor the day-to-day reality of the occupation many Israelis ignore, provided cameras to Nariman Tamimi and another relative in the village of Nabi Saleh.</p>
<p>Such videos have drawn international attention to Israel&#8217;s ongoing occupation and prompted conspiracy theories among far-right Israelis who maintain that every unflattering glimpse of daily realities in the occupied West Bank must be, somehow, staged for the cameras by Palestinians. After Ahed&#8217;s arrest, Israel&#8217;s former ambassador to the United States, Michael Oren, admitted that he had even <a href="https://theintercept.com/2018/01/25/israeli-official-mocked-bizarre-claim-detained-palestinian-teen-paid-actor/">conducted a secret investigation</a> into claims that the Tamimis are not a real family, but a troupe of actors paid to pretend that the occupation of their land upsets them.</p>
<p>Ahed <a href="https://www.timesofisrael.com/military-court-indicts-soldier-slapping-teen-ahed-tamimi-her-mother/">told the court</a> that her anger at the soldiers was motivated not by her mother&#8217;s camera, but the fact that their unit had just <a href="https://theintercept.com/2018/02/26/pre-dawn-raid-israel-arrests-badly-wounded-cousin-ahed-tamimi-jailed-protest-icon/">shot her 15-year-old cousin Mohammed</a> in the head during a protest.</p>
<p>Israeli military courts have <a href="https://972mag.com/the-idf-is-putting-palestinians-on-trial-for-facebook-posts/117910/">reportedly</a> charged dozens of young Palestinians with incitement based on their Facebook posts in the past three years. On Thursday, another young woman, <a href="https://twitter.com/noamr/status/976853881597628419">Yasmin Abu Srur</a> from Bethlehem, was reportedly sentenced to seven months in jail for a Facebook post.</p>

<p>The sentences were announced after the Tamimis accepted a plea deal to avoid spending even more time in detention during drawn-out legal proceedings. According to <a href="https://www.btselem.org/publications/summaries/201803_minors_in_jeopardy">a new report</a> from B&#8217;Tselem, Israel&#8217;s military court system has a nearly 100 percent conviction rate in large part because Palestinian defendants, particularly children, often agree to plead guilty after lengthy periods of pretrial detention. Ahed turned 17 during her time in custody.</p>

<p>The video of Azaria&#8217;s crime was recorded by <a href="https://www.npr.org/sections/parallels/2017/01/23/511230102/in-west-bank-witnesses-to-conflict-are-using-video-to-document-what-they-see">Imad Abu Shamsiyeh</a>, a Palestinian shoemaker who documents the activities of Israeli troops in Hebron.</p>
<p>The former director of B&#8217;Tselem, Jessica Montell, pointed out on Twitter that Thursday&#8217;s print edition of Haaretz reported Ahed Tamimi&#8217;s harsh sentence alongside another court&#8217;s ruling that Daniel Cohen Shor, a young Israeli who took part in the vicious beating of a Palestinian man, would only be punished with community service.</p>
