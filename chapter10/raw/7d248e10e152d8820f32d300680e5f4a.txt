  <p>Many of the inmates sentenced to death over a deadly sarin gas attack on the Japanese subway appear to be one step closer to their execution dates. </p><div class="inline-content wysiwyg right">
<div class="inner">
  <h2>Key points:</h2><ul><li>The sarin gas attack in Tokyo was the first act of terrorism using a weapon of mass destruction</li><li>Thirteen members of the Aum Shinrikyo cult are on death row</li><li>The Japan Society for Cult Prevention and Recovery believes we can learn from convicted cult members</li></ul></div>
</div><p> </p><p>But some victims believe most of the convicted Aum Shinrikyo cult members should have their lives spared, fearing they could become martyrs if they are executed. </p><p>Thirteen people died and more than 4,000 were injured 23 years ago today, when the doomsday cult carried out its deadly chemical weapons attack on the world's busiest metro system. </p><p>The cult had <a href="/news/2018-02-10/doomsday-cult-aum-shinrikyo-sarin-gas-tests-at-banjawarn-wa/9401216" target="_self" title="">prepared for the attack at a Banjawarn station, a remote sheep station it owned in Western Australia.</a> </p><p>Seven of the 13 inmates on death row have been transferred from the Tokyo detention centre to other facilities around the country — prompting speculation the Government is preparing to execute them. </p><p>Hiroyuki Nagaoka has dedicated his life to helping Aum Shinrikyo members escape the doomsday cult. </p><p>"I met [cult leader] Shoko Asahara 29 years ago — and I had a strong intuition that if we allow this group to go on they would do something very terrible," he said. </p><p>He helped his son escape the cult, which made him a target. </p><p>Aum members tried to assassinate him with VX nerve agent in January 1995 — they failed — but two months later the cult carried out its deadly attacks on the Tokyo subway. </p><div class="inline-content photo full">
    <a href="/news/2018-03-20/hiroyuki-nagaoka/9564974">











  
  
  
    
    
    
    
    
    
    
    
    
  <img src="http://www.abc.net.au/news/image/9564970-3x2-700x467.jpg" alt="Headshot of Hiroyuki Nagaoka who has oxygen hooked up to his nose. " title="Hiroyuki Nagaoka" width="700" height="467"/>
</a><a href="/news/2018-03-20/hiroyuki-nagaoka/9564974" class="inline-caption"><strong>
        Photo:</strong>
       Hiroyuki Nagaoka says Aum Shinrikyo recruited members with long sermons.  <span class="source">(ABC News: Jake Sturmer)</span>
      </a></div><p> </p><h2>Subway sarin attack </h2><p>Thirteen people died, 50 people were left permanently injured, and thousands of others were temporarily blinded by the gas in the 1995 attack. </p><p>Five men released the deadly nerve agent in carriages of crowded commuter trains during Tokyo's morning rush hour.</p><p>The sarin gas attack in Tokyo is regarded as the first ever use of a weapon of mass destruction in an act of terrorism. </p><p>The perpetrators of the crime thought they were carrying out a holy act in line with the beliefs of the doomsday cult. </p><p>The prosecutors of the criminal trials believe that day was chosen to divert the attention of police who were planning a raid on the cult's headquarters. </p><p>Asahara was found guilty of masterminding the attacks in 2004 and sentenced to death by hanging, but his execution was postponed while the appeals of his fellow criminals were heard. </p><p>He is now 63 years old, and spends his days in solitary confinement. </p><p>The appeal pathways for his fellow criminals were exhausted in January. </p><div class="inline-content photo full">
    <a href="/news/2016-09-08/shoko-asahara/7827722">











  
  
  
    
    
    
    
    
    
    
    
    
  <img src="http://www.abc.net.au/news/image/143156-3x2-700x467.jpg" alt="Shoko Asahara" title="Shoko Asahara" width="700" height="467"/>
</a><a href="/news/2016-09-08/shoko-asahara/7827722" class="inline-caption"><strong>
        Photo:</strong>
       The head of the cult, Shoko Asahara, was found guilty of masterminding the attacks in 2004. <span class="source">(Reuters)</span>
      </a></div><p> </p><h2>'Much to learn' from death row inmates </h2><p>Because the inmates are on death row, contact with them is severely limited. </p><p>Social psychologist Kimiaki Nishida performed psychological evaluations on many of the convicted cultists. </p><p>Little is understood about the motivations of Aum Shinrikyo, and he said there would be a huge opportunity to learn from them if their lives were spared. </p><p>"The question that I have is how, what triggered that change and how did their minds change?" he said. </p><p>"How did this transformation occur? And if I could find that out, it would be a great help in countering these various terrorist groups." </p><div class="inline-content photo full">
    <a href="/news/2018-03-20/kimiaki-nishida/9564976">











  
  
  
    
    
    
    
    
    
    
    
    
  <img src="http://www.abc.net.au/news/image/9564972-3x2-700x467.jpg" alt="Headshot of Kimiaki Nishida talking into a microphone. " title="Kimiaki Nishida" width="700" height="467"/>
</a><a href="/news/2018-03-20/kimiaki-nishida/9564976" class="inline-caption"><strong>
        Photo:</strong>
       Kimiaki Nishida is hoping to understand why people join the Amu Shinrikyo cult. <span class="source">(ABC News: Jake Sturmer)</span>
      </a></div><p> </p><p>He is chair of the Japan Society for Cult Prevention and Recovery, which wants 12 of the 13 convicted cultists to have their sentences commuted. </p><p>The society said it did not have a position on the execution of Asahara.</p><p>Mr Nagaoka is also a member of the society — and said Aum Shinrikyo managed to convince its victims with exceptionally long sermons that started early and finished late. </p><blockquote class="quote--pullquote"><p>"In such conditions you lose your ability to think," he said. </p></blockquote><p>They were brainwashed to think it was wonderful to kill people because it would help them and send their souls to heaven. </p><p>"They really believed — when I do go and meet them in detention centres they all say I want to die," he said. </p><p>"I always tell them don't be absurd, you have to tell your experience so the other members can return to normal beings who can think with their own heads." </p><p>Any decisions on clemency are up to the National Government's Cabinet. </p><p>Executions in Japan are cloaked in secrecy so it is impossible to predict exactly when any of the cult members will be hanged. </p><div class="inline-content video full">
