"Red dwarf systems, especially coolest red dwarfs, are just beginning to be investigated, so they are very exciting targets for future exoplanet research."</p><p>Researchers also discovered that planets orbiting red dwarf stars have a similar so-called radius gap that planets around solar-type stars are supposed to possess.</p><p>"This is a unique finding, and many theoretical astronomers are now investigating what causes this gap," Hirano added.</p><p>Hirano hopes that NASA’s Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite (TESS), slated to be launched in April 2018, will help alien planet-hunters discover more new planets.</p><p>"TESS is expected to find many candidate planets around bright stars closer to Earth," Hirano said. "This will greatly facilitate follow-up observations, including investigation of planetary atmospheres and determining the precise orbit of the planets."</p><p>The findings of the new research were <a href="http://iopscience.iop.org/article/10.3847/1538-3881/aaaa6e/meta" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">published </a>in <a href="http://iopscience.iop.org/article/10.3847/1538-3881/aaa9c1/meta" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">two </a>papers in the Astronomical Journal.</p></div></div></article></div></div><div id="block-ibtmedia-dfp-ibtmedia-dfp-belowcontent" class="block block-ibtmedia-dfp"><div class="content"><div id="rcjsload_ibtba"></div>
