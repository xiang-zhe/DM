<!-- ArticleText BGN --><div class="ArticleText">
<!--googleon:index-->
<p>Scientists have crystallized proteins in space to manufacture artificial cat blood for use in surgeries on the popular pet back down on Earth.</p>
<p>âThe animal medical front has struggled with a severe shortage of transfusion blood,â said Teruyuki Komatsu, professor of applied chemistry of Chuo University. âArtificial blood will contribute to animal health care.â</p>
<p>Proteins that can be used to make artificial blood crystallize much better in a gravity-free environment than they do on Earth, according to Komatsu.</p>
<p>Samples of serum albumin, proteins contained in the blood of cats, were crystallized in the crystal-growth facility aboard the Japanese experimental module Kibo of the International Space Station, scientists of the Japan Aerospace Exploration Agency and Chuo University announced on March 19.</p>
<p>Back on Earth, Japanese scientists analyzed the structure of the crystallized proteins.</p>
<p>By identifying the structure of the protein, the scientific team produced components that can be preserved as powder and utilized for artificial blood.</p>
<p>The components work with different blood types so it carries no risk of rejection.</p>
<p>The number of cats kept as pets surpassed that of dogs in Japan in 2017, according to the Japan Pet Food Association.</p>
<p>Despite the rise in the number of surgeries that require blood transfusions, a national system to stockpile blood to be infused into the animals has not been created.</p>
<p>Scientists are also developing artificial blood that can be infused into dogs.</p>
<p>The study team aims to put the artificial blood into practical use after confirming its safety.</p>
<p>The study results were published March 19 in the online edition of the British scientific journal Journal of Materials Chemistry B.</p>
